PATTERN A · 12 STATEMENTS
Anxious Attachment

After abuse, anxious attachment shows up as a heightened need for reassurance, fear of abandonment, and hypervigilance around other people's moods and reactions. Tick every statement that feels true for you now.

I need frequent reassurance from people I care about that everything is okay between us
When someone doesn't reply quickly, I spiral into fear that they are angry with me or pulling away
I overanalyse tone, wording, and body language in conversations for signs that something is wrong
I feel an intense fear of being abandoned — even in relationships where there is no real threat
I people-please instinctively — saying yes to keep the peace even when I mean no
I feel clingy or "too much" in relationships and worry that I push people away with my needs
My mood depends heavily on how the important people in my life are treating me that day
I apologise excessively — even when I haven't done anything wrong — to prevent conflict
I find it hard to be alone — silence or absence from others triggers anxiety quickly
I have stayed in or returned to relationships I knew were harmful because the alternative felt worse
I downplay my own needs and suppress my feelings to avoid burdening or losing people
I feel more comfortable when I can constantly monitor whether someone still values me
PATTERN B · 12 STATEMENTS
Avoidant Attachment

Avoidant attachment after abuse looks like emotional shutdown, compulsive self-reliance, and discomfort with closeness. It is your psyche's attempt to ensure you are never hurt that way again.

I feel deeply uncomfortable when people get emotionally close to me or depend on me
I pride myself on not needing anyone — and feel uneasy when I realise I do need someone
When relationships start to feel serious, I look for reasons to pull back or exit
I suppress and dismiss my emotions — feeling them fully seems dangerous or pointless
I find vulnerability in others irritating or overwhelming — I don't know how to respond to it
I keep people at arm's length — revealing very little of my real inner world to anyone
I am drawn to independence to the point where it isolates me — and I prefer it that way
When I'm stressed, I withdraw completely rather than reaching out for support
Commitment feels like a trap — I keep one foot out of relationships as an exit strategy
I intellectualise my feelings rather than actually feeling them — it feels safer that way
I feel most at ease in relationships that stay surface-level — depth makes me want to retreat
I struggle to ask for help — doing so feels like weakness or like giving someone power over me
PATTERN C · 12 STATEMENTS
Disorganised Attachment

Disorganised attachment is the most common outcome of narcissistic abuse. It is the painful contradiction of wanting closeness desperately while also fearing it deeply — a push-pull that leaves you exhausted and confused in relationships.

I want deep connection more than anything — but when I get close to someone, I start to panic
I sabotage relationships that are actually going well — as if I'm waiting for it to go wrong anyway
My feelings about the same person can shift dramatically — ideolising them one day, pushing them away the next
I have a deep internal sense that I am fundamentally unlovable or that something is wrong with me
I am attracted to people who are emotionally unavailable or chaotic — "safe" people feel boring or suspicious
I dissociate or emotionally shut down during conflict — I freeze, go blank, or feel nothing
I swing between clinging to someone and suddenly needing to get as far away from them as possible
Kindness from someone new makes me suspicious — I wait for the mask to drop
I feel safest when I have control over the emotional distance between me and others
My sense of self collapses under stress — I lose track of who I am when relationships become difficult
I find it hard to trust my own instincts about people — my judgement feels permanently broken
Love has always felt like something that comes with pain attached — I don't know if safe love is real
